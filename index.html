Background

Almost one million people in the UK live with heart failure, with almost 50% also experiencing kidney (renal) problems. The heart and kidneys are closely interconnected: if the heart suddenly becomes less efficient, renal function can worsen.

Modern treatments enable people with heart failure to live longer, healthier lives, but their medications must be carefully monitored. Too low a dose of heart medicine can worsen heart failure symptoms, while higher doses may impair renal function.

Both situations can increase the risk of hospital admission.

Trial Summary

The COMPUTATIONAL will explore the clinical and cost-effectiveness of a personalised renal function monitoring algorithm, designed to prevent hospital admissions due to renal impairment without worsening heart failure outcomes.

The algorithm, classed as a medical device, consists of a prediction model designed to tailor monitoring schedules, together with a decision tool that provides guidance on immediate interventions and recommendations for the primary care team.

Large Scale Study

200 GP practices across England evaluating around 20,000 patients

Randomised

100 centres using prediction model vs. 100 centres with standard care

Comprehensive Monitoring

18-month period with PROMs collected at baseline, 6 and 18 months

Study Design: We will recruit 200 GP practices across England that use the OPTUM (EMIS) system, evaluating around 20,000 patients. The trial will run for 18 months, in line with National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines and current standards of care.

Objectives

Our comprehensive research framework designed to evaluate the effectiveness and implementation of personalised renal function monitoring

Primary Objective

To compare the clinical effectiveness of personalised renal function monitoring with standard care in patients living with heart failure

Secondary Objectives

To determine if implementation of personalised renal function monitoring in patients living with heart failure improves a variety of clinical outcomes, when compared with standard care

To identify barriers in the optimal use of personalised renal function monitoring for both the primary care team and patients

Economic Objective

To estimate the cost-effectiveness of personalised renal function monitoring in comparison to standard care
